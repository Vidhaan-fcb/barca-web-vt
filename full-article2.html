Barça’s Asia Tour Meltdown: Chaos, Contracts & a Last-Minute Rescue
What was meant to be a triumphant pre-season tour through Asia for FC Barcelona turned into a chaotic saga of last-minute cancellations, legal threats, and an urgent financial bailout that nearly derailed the club’s global brand efforts
Back to News
The Build-Up: A High-Stakes Tour
Barcelona’s 2025 summer tour was set to reestablish their international footprint after years of financial restructuring. The club announced a series of friendly matches in Japan and South Korea, with major fixtures including: July 27 – vs. Vissel Kobe (Japan) July 31 – vs. FC Seoul (South Korea) August 4 – vs. Daegu FC (South Korea) With Marcus Rashford freshly signed, and the renovated Camp Nou still under construction, the tour was a vital opportunity for global fan engagement and commercial exposure.
Back to News
Sudden Collapse: The Kobe Cancellation
Just three days before the Kobe match, Barcelona canceled their trip to Japan, citing a “serious contractual breach”. The breach was traced to Yasuda Group, a Japanese promoter tasked with handling logistics and financial guarantees. According to club sources, Yasuda failed to transfer between €5–8 million, which Barcelona considered critical for match operations, player logistics, and insurance. As a result, Barça pulled out of the match, canceled their outbound flight, and instructed players to remain in Barcelona—prompting confusion and outrage among Japanese fans and organizers.

The Fallout: Blame and Legal Threats
The backlash was immediate. Albert Luque, representing D‑Drive (Barcelona’s Asian tour partner), blamed Yasuda Group for “breaching multiple financial agreements” and announced plans for legal action. In a press conference, Luque labeled the situation “an embarrassment,” adding that Barça had every right to protect itself financially. Fans in Japan, who had already purchased match tickets and merchandise, expressed frustration on social media, with local media questioning how such a premier club could be caught in what looked like amateur-hour logistics. Meanwhile, Spanish media outlets criticized Barcelona’s own administrative handling of the situation, with Cadena SER calling the entire episode “a disaster of coordination” and warning that it “tarnished Barça’s global image.”

The Rescue: Rakuten Steps In
In a stunning twist, former Barça shirt sponsor Rakuten reportedly stepped in to save the tour. According to multiple sources, Rakuten—keen to maintain its relationship with the club—fronted or facilitated the required payment to Yasuda Group or directly to cover Barça’s financial demands. By July 25, less than 48 hours after the cancellation, Barça reinstated the match against Vissel Kobe, rebooked their travel plans, and confirmed that Rashford and the squad would travel to Japan. The friendly was back on. Barcelona departed for Kobe immediately, and the remainder of the Asia tour schedule remained unchanged.

What This Means for the Club
While the team avoided a public relations disaster, the incident revealed the fragile nature of Barcelona’s financial and operational foundations. Despite a world-class brand, the club is still recovering from heavy debts and remains extremely dependent on sponsors, promoters, and licensing agreements to sustain major events like these. Additionally, the episode raised questions about due diligence and whether club leadership had vetted Yasuda Group adequately. It also underscored how tight Barcelona’s margins are—unable to risk even a few million in lost payments without canceling high-profile fixtures.

Rashford’s Debut and Moving Forward
Amid the chaos, Marcus Rashford’s debut became a beacon of hope. The English forward was able to join training in Kobe and is expected to make his first appearance for the club in the July 27 match against Vissel. Club president Joan Laporta later stated, “The situation was unfortunate, but the team is focused and united. Rashford is already making a strong impression.” Barça’s Asia tour will continue into South Korea, with matches against FC Seoul and Daegu FC, helping the club regain momentum and attention after the early misfire.

Final Thoughts
What began as a PR disaster for FC Barcelona turned into a cautionary tale of modern football’s dependence on third-party promoters and razor-thin financial planning. While Rakuten’s intervention salvaged the trip, the episode should serve as a wake-up call for the club’s leadership. Barcelona survived the crisis—but barely.
